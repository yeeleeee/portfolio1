Prototpying feedback pattern
target users: professors, instructional designers
target users' target users: students
goal: simplify the question authoring process without compromising learning principles behind it.
According to learning science, feedback is a crucial part of problem-solving especially in online learning materials, since they cannot get one in person. And obviously, giving a tailored feedback based on students' answer is far more effective than just showing the right answer. However, as ideal as it sounds, it is a very long and tedious process to make a perfect question. When the format of question gets more complex, such as "select all" or "put in order," feedback choices gets exponential.
for a 4-choice question, the number of possible answers (=feedback) is 4; for a "select all" question, it's 16; for "ordering" question, it's 24.
It's too much!
pain: it is very tedious to put feedback per choice, especially for "select all" questions.
Giving a feedback was where most users dropped out because the users either 1) gave up the feedback part and just continue with question making, or 2) gave up the whole question part, which is worse.
solution: we made two modes to minimize the drop out rate and give the most flexibility to the feedback process.
We first made a "simple" mode, where the professors could just put in right and wrong feedback. This is to minimize the dropout rate and to give students a feedback at least.
When the users toggle an "advanced" mode, they could specify the case where the students get wrong the most. We implemented the case-based solution because of the 1)cost of development, and 2) ease of users. Professors could specify the case where the students get wrong the most and give an instant feedback to the specific case.
Accounting for edge cases
While this works for the majority of the occasions, it doesn't for certain cases, such as when a student puts in the answer that is not common. To account for those cases, we ideated the option to notify the TA when a student puts a specific answer.
Process-A/B Testing
Going through an A/B testing. We needed to find the best point of compromise between development efficiency vs. user experience vs. learning principles.
